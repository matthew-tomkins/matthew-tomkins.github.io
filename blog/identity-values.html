IDENTITY, VALUES + STRENGTHS

MY CULTURE & INFLUENCES

I grew up in Red Beach on the Hibiscus Coast, about half an hour north of central Auckland. It was a safe, easygoing community - beaches everywhere, and a real sense of freedom. This was the 90s, pre-social media and smartphones, when life felt a lot simpler. Cultural influence came mainly from the people around me - friends, family, the local community - and externally from what was on national TV and magazines.

My friend group back then had its own strong values. We were a bit alternative, drawn to music, skating, surf culture - basically, living life for the fun of it and not following the crowd. Those friends are still really important to me today. They shaped so much of who I am and how I see the world.

Being Pākehā and growing up in a very white family, I took a lot for granted. My parents and older brother are deeply kind hearted people, and I see how much of their influence I carry with me in my day to day - particularly in how I care for others and accept people from all walks of life. That said, it did take time to recognise how narrow my early lens was. Over the years, I’ve made a conscious effort to broaden that perspective beyond the default of being a white male in Aotearoa, and it’s something I will always keep working on.

Sport and music played a huge part in my childhood. I was an undiagnosed ADHD kid with energy to burn, and sport gave me a place to channel that. I was naturally athletic and always did well, and I think that’s where teamwork and shared success first became important values for me. At the same time, I played the trumpet through my school years - was pretty gifted at it too - and being in the concert band in high school taught me how to listen, contribute, and aim for excellence in a shared space.

As I got older and the internet really took off, so did my exposure to new cultures, ideas, and movements. For a while I leaned into that - absorbing new ways of thinking, especially around identity, gender, and race. It helped me understand how narrow our personal perspectives can be, and the importance of stepping back, listening, and being open to challenge. Today I feel internet culture has shifted away from authenticity to algorithms and click-bait. The messages are still there and available but diluted from artificially created content and narratives.

These days, having entered my 40s, I rely more on my own compass. Friends, family, and community are still vital to me, but I’ve come to trust my own judgement more. I’m guided by the values shaped in my early life - kindness, creativity, fairness, inclusion - and keep refining them as I grow. The past gave me the tools, and now I use them to navigate life in a way that feels true to who I am today.

PERSONAL STRENGTHS

I identify my core strengths as communication, empathy, work ethic, creativity, professional experience, and project management, as key in supporting my learning journey. Effective communication allows me to collaborate well with peers and facilitators, ensuring I stay engaged and can express ideas clearly. Empathy helps me understand others’ perspectives, fostering better collaboration and deeper comprehension as well as wanting to see success in those around me. My proven strong work ethic will keep me focused and disciplined, enabling me to push through challenges and stay on track.

Creativity will help me approach problems from innovative angles, while my professional experience gives me a solid foundation to apply new knowledge in practical scenarios. Additionally, my project management skills will ensure I stay organised, prioritise tasks effectively, and meet deadlines. These core strengths collectively will allow me to execute tasks efficiently, collaborate effectively with others, and remain adaptable and persistent in achieving my goals.


PERSONAL LIMITATIONS

My limitations, such as self-doubt, putting others ahead of myself to my own detriment, overconfidence at times, and mental wellbeing, can certainly affect my learning and career development. Self-doubt has potential to hold me back, making it difficult to trust my abilities, which can prevent me from fully engaging with new opportunities. I sometimes hesitate to take action for fear of failure, limiting my potential before I even get started.

Additionally, putting others needs before my own can leave me feeling drained and distracted, impacting my ability to focus on my own growth. On the flip side, overconfidence can lead me to take on more than I can handle or overlook important details, which can hinder progress. Finally, general mental wellbeing can make it harder to maintain the energy and clarity needed to stay consistent in my learning journey. These limitations combined can slow my progress, but I am aware of them and am working to manage them to ensure they don’t prevent me from achieving my goals.

PRODUCTIVITY RESISTANCE + TENSION

In a previous role, I was rapidly promoted from entry-level to management after returning to the company. I found myself overseeing communications, compliance, and scheduling across multiple divisions, including one led by a close friend of mine. I was eager to support his success, but my promotion inadvertently impacted his role, as I took on some of his managerial duties due to his struggles with managing expectations. This created tension, as he felt I was taking away opportunities from him, even though I was brought in to help alleviate his workload.

To address this, I tried offering emotional and professional support, dedicating my personal time to listen, strategise, and offer advice. I thought this would help him manage his demanding workload, but over time, the situation became more strained. Despite keeping communication open and being there for him, it became clear he felt frustrated and blamed me for his lack of progress. Even after directly discussing the issue, he dismissed my concerns, which left me feeling stuck.

In hindsight, I realise that while I was right to be empathetic, I should have established clearer boundaries between professionalism and personal support. I now understand that recognising the friction earlier and addressing it directly could have helped protect my own emotional investment. While the outcome might not have changed, setting firmer boundaries would have allowed me to maintain a healthier balance between my personal and professional roles.

ETHICAL DECISION

In 2013 my grandmother, at 94 years old, suffered a severe stroke that left her immobile and unable to speak. Despite her tough and independent nature, having previously defied the odds after a heart attack, this stroke was different. She was no longer the lively, independent woman who zoomed around her neighbourhood on a mobility scooter, but instead, she faced the harsh reality of being reliant on others for everything. The doctors told us she might have a chance to recover, but it would mean a life of immobility and constant care. As a family, we were ready to provide her with whatever support she needed, but we had to respect her wishes.

As she lay in the hospital, unable to communicate verbally, she made it clear that she didn’t want to live that life. Through her actions - removing her IV line and refusing medication - she was signalling that she had made her decision. It wasn’t an easy one for us, but we understood that she had lived life on her terms and this was no different. After discussing it with the family and the medical staff we made the decision to support her. It was heartbreaking, but we respected her choice, knowing it was her right to choose how she wanted to spend her final days.

To this day I still admire the courage and strength she showed throughout the process. Her decision was made with clarity, and her resolve was something I will never forget. In the days that followed she passed away peacefully at my uncle’s home, surrounded by family, just as she had lived - fearlessly and on her own terms. I reflect on that experience with deep respect for her and pride in our family’s ability to honour her wishes, even through such immense grief. Her strength continues to inspire me, and I carry the lessons of her fierce independence and wisdom with me.
